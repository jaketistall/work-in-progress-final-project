Fish and Trout of Utah
Utah boasts a variety of trout species, each found in unique habitats and recognizable by distinct features. Some of the different types of trout were already here in utah when we got here and some were introduced by pioneers and are non-native. Each type of trout has something special to offer and looks distinct.


Trout identification process

  1. Look at the colors of the fish, different colors can help identify the type of fish
  2. Know what time of year the different trout species spawn, and if they are able to spawn
  3. Know the different bodies of water that certain speicies can be found in
  4. Look for cuts on the throat of the fish
  5. Look for the spawning colors of the fish and the ways that the trout will act different when spawning

Different trout species

rainbow trout

Rainbow trout, the most common, are silvery with a pink lateral stripe and black spots on their tails and backs, thriving in places like Strawberry Reservoir and the Provo River. They are very common and the most likely to come across. The rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) is a popular and widely recognized freshwater fish known for its vibrant colors and distinctive pink stripe running along its sides. Native to the cold-water tributaries of the Pacific Ocean in Asia and North America, rainbow trout have been extensively introduced to various habitats worldwide due to their appeal to anglers and their adaptability to different environments. In Utah, these fish thrive in numerous bodies of water, including rivers, lakes, and reservoirs. Key river systems where rainbow trout are commonly found include the Green River, Provo River, and Weber River. Additionally, they are prevalent in many of Utah's lakes and reservoirs, such as Strawberry Reservoir, Flaming Gorge Reservoir, and Fish Lake. These bodies of water provide the cold, clean, oxygen-rich environments that rainbow trout need to flourish, making Utah a prime destination for fishing enthusiasts.

Brown trout

Brown trout are golden-brown with dark spots surrounded by light halos and are common in colder streams such as the Green River. Brook trout, smaller and dark-bodied with light wavy patterns and red fins edged in white, favor high-elevation streams like those in the Uinta Mountains. The brown trout (Salmo trutta) is a versatile and resilient freshwater fish species renowned for its golden-brown color, dark spots, and robust body. Originally native to Europe and western Asia, brown trout have been successfully introduced to many regions across the globe due to their hardiness and popularity among anglers. In Utah, brown trout inhabit a variety of aquatic environments, thriving in both rivers and reservoirs. Notable river systems where brown trout can be found include the Provo River, Weber River, and Logan River. These rivers provide the cold, clear, and well-oxygenated waters that brown trout favor. Additionally, brown trout are present in several of Utah's lakes and reservoirs, such as Strawberry Reservoir, Flaming Gorge Reservoir, and Jordanelle Reservoir. These diverse habitats offer ideal conditions for brown trout, supporting their growth and making Utah a favored location for fishing enthusiasts seeking this prized species.


Bonneville cutthroat trout

The native Bonneville cutthroat trout, marked by a reddish-orange slash under its jaw, lives in areas like the Weber and Bear River drainages. The Bonneville cutthroat trout (Oncorhynchus clarkii utah) is a distinct subspecies of cutthroat trout named after ancient Lake Bonneville, which once covered much of present-day Utah. Characterized by its vibrant colors and the distinctive red or orange slash marks under its jaw, this trout is native to the Bonneville Basin and is highly prized for its historical and ecological significance. In Utah, Bonneville cutthroat trout can be found in various river systems and lakes, especially those that retain the cold, clear, and well-oxygenated waters they require. Prominent river systems supporting this species include the Logan River, Bear River, and the Weber River. Additionally, they inhabit numerous lakes and reservoirs such as Strawberry Reservoir, Bear Lake, and the streams and lakes in the Uinta Mountains. Conservation efforts have been essential in maintaining and restoring the populations of Bonneville cutthroat trout, ensuring that they continue to thrive in their native habitats and remain a cherished species for anglers and wildlife enthusiasts alike.


Brook trout

The brook trout (Salvelinus fontinalis) is a beautiful and highly prized freshwater fish known for its vibrant colors, featuring a dark green to brown body adorned with light spots, and a distinctive marbled pattern along its back and dorsal fin. Native to the eastern United States and Canada, brook trout have been introduced to many parts of North America, including Utah, where they thrive in cold, clear, and well-oxygenated waters. In Utah, brook trout can be found in numerous mountain streams, rivers, and high-elevation lakes. Prominent river systems and lakes where brook trout are commonly found include the Uinta Mountains' streams, Boulder Mountain lakes, and the Logan River. Additionally, they inhabit several alpine lakes and reservoirs, such as Mirror Lake and Trial Lake. These cold-water habitats provide the ideal conditions for brook trout, allowing them to flourish and support a dynamic fishing environment for anglers seeking the challenge and beauty of this species.

Tiger trout

The tiger trout, a striking hybrid of brook and brown trout, features a marbled pattern and thrives in reservoirs like Scofield.
Golden trout, a rare and vibrant species, have bright yellow-golden scales, red lateral stripes, and faint parr marks; they are stocked in select high-altitude alpine lakes in the Uintas. The tiger trout (Salmo trutta × Salvelinus fontinalis) is a striking hybrid species resulting from the crossbreeding of a brown trout and a brook trout. Known for its unique and bold vermiculated pattern resembling a tiger’s stripes, the tiger trout is both visually distinctive and a strong predator, making it a favorite among anglers. In Utah, tiger trout have been stocked in various water bodies to control rough fish populations and enhance sport fishing opportunities. These hybrids are typically found in reservoirs and lakes where they can thrive in the cooler, deeper waters. Notable bodies of water in Utah where tiger trout are commonly found include Scofield Reservoir, Huntington Reservoir, and Panguitch Lake. These environments offer the appropriate conditions that support the growth and sustainability of tiger trout, providing anglers with exciting fishing opportunities due to the fish's aggressive nature and remarkable fighting ability.


Golden trout

a rare and vibrant species, have bright yellow-golden scales, red lateral stripes, and faint parr marks; they are stocked in select high-altitude alpine lakes in the Uintas. Identifying trout involves recognizing their unique colors, patterns, and habitats. The golden trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss aguabonita) is a stunning and rare freshwater fish, renowned for its vibrant golden-yellow coloration and striking red lateral stripes. Originally native to the high-altitude streams and lakes of the Sierra Nevada in California, golden trout are celebrated for their beauty and are considered one of the most visually striking trout species. In Utah, golden trout have been introduced to select high-elevation lakes and streams to enhance fishing opportunities. These fish thrive in the cold, clear, and pristine waters typically found at higher altitudes. Key locations in Utah where golden trout can be found include the remote lakes and streams of the Uinta Mountains and Boulder Mountain. These high-altitude habitats provide the necessary conditions that golden trout require, such as low temperatures and high water quality. Anglers seeking the unique experience of catching golden trout often venture into these secluded and scenic areas, where the beauty of the fish matches the stunning natural surroundings.
